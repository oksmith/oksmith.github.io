I've been interested in trees since about last year when I was in between jobs, I spent a lot of time paying attention to all of the different tree species in my area.

In spring of this year, I started trying to test myself to see how many species I could recognise. I'd often bump into a tree I can't identify, and start doing some online digging to figure out what it is.

But then I thought - surely someone has tracked this data somewhere? 

It turns out there's a public database of over a million trees in London, and the data is free to download from [this website](https://data.london.gov.uk/dataset/london-public-realm-trees-2r45m). This post will be a bit of a rundown of all the things I learned about trees from browsing online and digging into this dataset.


## What is the London Tree Map?


The _London Public Realm Tree Map_ is a dataset put together by GiGL (Greenspace Information for Greater London) on behalf of the Greater London Authority.

It's a list of over a million trees on public land across London, with details on species, location, size, and age. Data comes from all London boroughs, plus the City of London, Transport for London, the Royal Parks, and a few other public bodies who between them are responsible for maintaining the trees.

The November 2025 release is the most detailed version yet. It also, for the first time, includes 2090 climate suitability scores for every species. More on that shortly.

It's worth knowing what the dataset _doesn't_ cover though. Because there are actually more than 8 million trees in London. If you've got an apple tree in your garden, it won't be in there. Private gardens, railway land, corporate grounds are all excluded from the dataset. The dataset covers roughly 14% of London's tree population, which is still plenty to sink my teeth into for data analysis purposes.

Data quality varies quite a bit depending on which borough you're looking at. Some have done a meticulous job — every tree logged with species, age, girth, and condition. Others have uploaded records with little more than a rough location and a vague genus name.

My guess is that tree data collection in London isn't governed by a single standard — each borough manages its own inventory, each with its own surveyors and and processes (and its own budget!). I'll talk more about data quality later on, but for now let's talk about how London's [urban forest](https://www.london.gov.uk/programmes-strategies/environment-and-climate-change/parks-green-spaces-and-biodiversity/trees-and-woodlands/london-urban-forest-resource-hub) is composed.

The most common tree in London's public realm is, (surprisingly for me), Cherry. Or more precisely it is _Prunus_, the genus that covers cherries, plums, and ornamental blossom trees.

The reason it's such a common public tree is partly because it's genuinely popular as a street tree, and partly due to a data quality issue I discovered. Tens of thousands of records are filed simply as "Prunus" with no further detail, lumping dozens of distinct species into one bucket. 🤦‍♂️ I'll talk more about this later in the data quality section.

London Plane comes in a close second. This is the huge, towering tree most people picture when they think of a classic London street. They provide plenty of shade and are excellent at [removing harmful pollution](https://www.sciencefocus.com/planet-earth/do-london-plane-trees-actually-absorb-pollution-into-their-bark) from London's air.

Next on the list is the Oak (the national tree! 🇬🇧) followed by Ash.

That Ash figure is worth pausing on. I remember hearing about [Ash dieback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hymenoscyphus_fraxineus) a long time ago but I never really absorbed what it meant at the time. It's a disease that arrived in the UK in about 2012, and is slowly but surely working its way through the country's ash population at an 85% mortality rate. Over 58,000 ash trees in London's public realm alone represent a significant future liability for councils — many of these trees will need replacing over the coming decades, and there's really not much we can do about it.

One interesting thing I found out was what "DBH" means. It stands for Diameter at Breast Height, and is the standard measurement of a tree's trunk diameter. By breast height, it means approximately 1.3m tall, but it varies depending on the country.

You can also use a tree's DBH measurement to estimate its age! You just divide the DBH by a species-specific denominator (growth factor). The reason it works is because, within a species, the relationship between DBH and age is pretty linear. A 50cm DBH oak tree will be roughly twice the age of a 25cm DBH oak of the same species (assuming they are grown in similar conditions).

Next I'll dig into the "climate suitability" field in the London Public Realm database, and what it means for the future of London's urban forest.


## Climate Suitability of London's Trees


The November 2025 dataset includes something new: a climate suitability score for every species in the dataset, assessed against what London's climate is projected to look like in 2090.

The study was commissioned by the GLA and the Forestry Commission, and delivered by Urban Plant Lab. [This is the original paper](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/398319734_UPLLondonReportNov25AW_1-compressed) (it has some nice visuals and implications on policy, give it a read!). 

The researchers identified "climate analogues", places in the world that already have the climate London is heading towards, and assessed whether each species currently exists and thrives in those places. They combined this with other plant trait analysis to understand how well each species tolerates heat, drought, and other climate stresses.

## The Data Quality Problem

Before any analysis was possible, the data needed cleaning. Two problems stood out immediately.

### The Cherry Problem

The most common "species" in the dataset is Prunus — the genus that covers cherries, plums, almonds, and ornamental blossom trees. Over 81,000 records are filed at genus level only, with no further identification. A surveyor looked at a tree, wrote "Prunus", and moved on. This makes Prunus appear to be the single most common tree in London, which might be true but is practically not very useful.

Interestingly, the Urban Plant Lab report flagged exactly the same issue in their own analysis — a reassuring sign that this isn't just me being pedantic.

The same issue appears with "Malus" (Apple / Crab Apple) and "Tilia" (Lime). All have a significant number of rows logged at the genus level. For the charts in this post, I've relabelled these records as "Cherry / Plum (unspecified)\*" and "Lime (unspecified)\*". Hammersmith and Fulham stands out as the worst offender for unspecified Prunus records — over 22% of their trees are logged only to genus level. Kensington and Chelsea (16%) and Redbridge (15%) follow close behind. 

### Plane vs London Plane

A subtler problem: some boroughs record their Plane trees as "London Plane", others simply as "Plane". A quick borough-by-borough breakdown confirmed this is entirely a naming convention difference: boroughs either use one name or the other, never both. All Plane variants have been consolidated to "London Plane" throughout.

### Southwark's weird flex

While exploring the size data, something strange jumped out: 96 of the top 100 largest trunk girths in the entire dataset — across all 33 London boroughs — belong to trees in Southwark. Apparently Southwark is home to an extraordinary concentration of enormous trees.

Except it.... probably isn't. My speculation is that this is a units error or other some kind of system error for whoever took those surveys.

Rather than attempt to correct the values (which would require knowing which records are wrong and which are genuinely large trees) I just treated any Southwark girth value above 120cm as suspicious, and removed it from the analysis. That threshold was chosen by looking at the 99.5th percentile of girth values across all other boroughs.

Another, more local, detail caught my eye. In Southwark, there are no trees recorded in Elephant Park — the development built on the site of the demolished Heygate Estate. Since demolition began in 2014, this suggests Southwark's survey data is from before that year (at least for that area). I can tell you firsthand that there are plenty of trees in the Elephant Park area now, and all are missing from the dataset.

### Missing data by borough

Beyond species naming, the dataset has gaps in some key fields — age, girth, height, canopy. These gaps are not evenly distributed across boroughs. The heatmap below shows completeness by borough across the main fields:

## A visual map of the trees in Burgess Park

I wanted to build a map of my own. Plotly (my favourite Python package for generating visualisations) lets you plot scatter maps, given a bunch of coordinates (all latitudes/longitudes are present in the dataset!). I also came across a very cool package, [osmnx](https://osmnx.readthedocs.io/), that lets you fetch the boundary polygon of any named place from OpenStreetMap. So rather than manually defining a bounding box, I could ask it for the exact shape of Burgess Park and filter the dataset to only trees within it.

I chose to map out Burgess Park as it's close to home and I go on runs there all the time.
